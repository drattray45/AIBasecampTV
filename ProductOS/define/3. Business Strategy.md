# Business Strategy Framework

Web search was unavailable this session, so competitor/anchor prices are tagged `[confirm live]` and cost figures are `[working assumption — re-ground with real data post-launch]`.

**Strategy summary (10-second read):** A creator-led digital-education business with **MacWhisper-style economics** (near-zero marginal cost, ~85–90% margin) and **Designjoy-style audience-as-moat**. Revenue model: **one-time digital products** (no subscription at launch). Ladder: free Starter Kit → **$17 tripwire** → **$99 founding → $149 → $199 flagship course** (payment plan at the top), all under Diane's **$299 ceiling**. North star is **sequenced**: weekly *qualified* email subscribers pre-launch, weekly buyers + subscriber→buyer conversion the moment the first pack ships. Moats: **counter-position to get in, build the version-dated Basecamp Path to stay in.**

## 1. Revenue Model

*How does the money flow from user to you?*

> Good: one primary model matched to your cost structure and category expectations
> Bad: freemium-by-default, two models in parallel, subscription on a one-shot product

**Common models — pick one:**

| Model | When it fits | Watch out for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Recurring subscription | Continuous-value tools (SaaS, mobile apps used daily) | Needs retention work; churn kills you |
| One-time purchase | Bounded-utility tools (Mac apps, calculators, generators) | No expansion revenue; need volume |
| Lifetime deal (LTD) | New launches needing cash + evangelists fast | Cap seats and time-box; otherwise you give away future MRR |
| Bring Your Own Key (BYOK) | AI apps with heavy API costs | Smaller addressable market — power users only |
| Productized service | Outcomes that need a human in the loop | You're capacity-constrained; productize ruthlessly |
| Take-rate / marketplace | Networks with two sides | Brutal cold-start; only viable with distribution |
| Free + affiliate / ads | Directories, content sites, free tools | Slow to monetize; need huge traffic |

**Your answer:** **One-time digital products** — a single primary model, sold as a ladder of discrete, finishable purchases (free Starter Kit → low-ticket pack → flagship course). Chosen over subscription for two reasons: (1) it matches Diane's **buy-once psychology** — a recurring charge is a visible, re-decided-monthly statement line that reintroduces the very shame she's hiding; (2) beginners are the **churniest possible membership base** because the brand explicitly promises they'll *graduate* — selling them recurring access contradicts the product's soul. **Membership is a deliberate Phase-2 retention lever**, not a v1 model: it only earns its keep once the pitch shifts from "learn the basics" to "stay current as the tools keep changing" (a real recurring need, but a later one). Running both at launch = the Two-Model Trap (discrete products vs. content treadmill pull in opposite directions). One model now.

## 2. Pricing Ladder

*What are your tiers, anchors, and price points?*

> Good: 2 or 3 tiers with the middle engineered as the default, annual discounted 20–30%, tested upward at least once
> Bad: single flat price, more than 3 tiers, "Enterprise — contact us" with no anchor, pricing chosen by gut

**Common shapes — pick one:**

| Shape | Example | Used by |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consumer mobile | Free → $9.99/mo or $59.99/yr | Most $10–50K MRR mobile apps |
| Prosumer / B2B starter | $19 / $49 / $99 monthly | Most $20–100K MRR B2B SaaS |
| Agency / team | $99 / $299 / $700 monthly | Most $100K+ MRR B2B SaaS |
| LTD launch | $79 / $199 / $299 lifetime, capped seats | LTD launches via AppSumo / RocketHub |
| BYOK / one-time | $49–149 one-time | Mac apps, desktop AI tools |

**Your answer:** A one-time-purchase ladder, each rung with a named job:

| Rung | Offer | Price | Job | Built for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | **Starter Kit** (lead magnet) | Free | Email capture + first frictionless home-turf win (Basecamp Path task 1) | Diane — top of funnel |
| 1 | **Tripwire pack** ("The 5 AI Tasks") | **$17 one-time** | Flip subscriber → first-ever buyer (the scarce, high-leverage conversion); doubles as cheapest market test | Diane — impulse, zero deliberation |
| 2 | **Flagship course** (full Basecamp Path) | **$99 founding → $149 → $199**, payment plan 3×$59 at top | The core capability transformation | Diane — her main buy, safely under $299 |
| 3 | *(edge, optional)* live/cohort or team version | **$399–$499** | Higher-touch / deductible | **NOT Diane** — freelancer/owner or employer |

The **$17 tripwire is the engineered default move**, not the flagship: turning a free subscriber into someone who has *paid once* is the single biggest lift to later flagship conversion (the first dollar is psychological, not financial — it flips "audience" to "customer," and every buyer becomes proof + a warm flagship lead + a conversion data point). Priced at $17 (not $27) to **maximize the count crossing the paid threshold**, not dollars/transaction; not under $9 (that signals junk). **Read its subscriber→buyer rate as a market-test signal** — if it converts, that's the green light to build the flagship. Flagship launches as a **$99 founding cohort** to buy the missing element (proof) fast — framed as *"founding members get the lowest price this will ever be, in exchange for feedback and an honest before/after testimonial"* (founder framing, **not** a launch discount, so $99 doesn't anchor the product forever; testimonial is a **stated term of entry, contractual, not a hope**). Ladder then steps $99 → $149 → toward the $299 ceiling as proof accumulates. No annual discount (that's a subscription lever); the equivalent pressure-release is **founding price + payment plan**. Rung 3 deliberately sits *above* $299 because charging more changes *who* you're selling to (freelancer/employer, not Diane). **Anchors confirmed June 21, 2026:** LinkedIn Learning $39.99/mo (~$240/yr annual), Coursera Plus $59/mo or $399/yr, MasterClass $120/$180/$240/yr, Udemy courses $15–200; on-wedge comps — Heather Murray module values ~£195/$248, Section crash course ~$600. A $99–199 one-time flagship sits *below a year of any subscription anchor* and reads as a finishable, owned purchase — well-placed under Diane's $299 ceiling.

## 3. Cost & Margin

*What does each paying user cost you to serve?*

> Good: per-user variable cost under 30% of revenue, hard usage caps per tier, fixed costs covered by current MRR
> Bad: unbounded AI costs, fixed costs growing faster than revenue, you can't recite your cost-per-user from memory

**Cost breakdown:**

```
Variable per-sale cost  =  payment processing (Stripe ~3%)
                        +  course-platform fee (~2%)
                        +  refund reserve (~5–10%, generous 60-day guarantee)
                        ≈  ~10–15% all-in
```

```
Fixed monthly cost  =  email/course platform [confirmed Jun 2026: Kit free <1K subs then $25/$50;
                                                host: Teachable $29–139, Skool $9/$99, Kajabi $143–399]
                    +  domain / hosting (trivial)
                    +  Scout / faceless-video production (your time or a contractor — the line that actually matters)
```

**Gross margin** = (revenue − variable cost) ÷ revenue.

**Your answer:** `[working assumption — re-ground post-launch]` **MacWhisper economics, not AI-app economics** — there is no per-user token/compute cost. All-in variable cost per sale ≈ **10–15%** (Stripe + platform + refund reserve), so **gross margin ≈ 85–90%**. Margin is **not the risk.** The real cost is **front-loaded content production** (Scout/faceless video) — a *fixed acquisition cost* paid up front in time/money, not a per-user cost. Therefore the binding constraint on this business is **distribution and conversion, not unit economics.** The one fixed line that matters is Scout production capacity (your time or a contractor); everything else is rounding. **Confirmed June 21, 2026: you can launch on ~$0–29/mo of tooling** (Kit free under 1K subs + a cheap host like Teachable Starter $29 or Skool Hobby $9) — fixed costs are genuinely not a constraint; Kajabi is overkill at launch.

## 4. Unfair Advantage

*Why can't a vibe-coded clone kill you in 6 months?*

> Good: a moat that compounds with time and use — distribution density, niche expertise, audience, switching costs, or counter-positioning
> Bad: "I built it first", "our AI is smarter", "we have better UX", "our team"

**Common unfair advantages:**

| Advantage type | What it looks like in the data |
| --- | --- |
| Distribution density | You own a channel (subreddit, creator network, App Store keyword cluster) |
| Niche expertise | You're the customer — insider knowledge competitors can't fake |
| Audience / brand | Built before launch; followers don't churn |
| Switching costs | Data lock-in, integrations, workflow embeddedness |
| Cross-product portfolio | Same buyer, multiple SKUs |
| Open-source community | Contributor flywheel |
| Counter-positioning | A model incumbents can't copy without cannibalizing themselves |

**Your answer:** Sequenced by horizon, not ranked — **counter-position to get in, build the Path to stay in.**

- **Entry card (strongest at launch, but imitable): counter-positioning.** The calm, jargon-free, "you're-not-behind" channel in a niche full of hype-bros and complexity. It's what makes you legible and distinct on day one, wins the click, and earns the subscribe. Structurally defended at the *incumbent* level — technical AI YouTubers and hustle-creators **cannot** chase the scared beginner without alienating their existing audience or diluting their brand — but a *new* entrant could choose the same lane, so it's ownable, not fenced.
- **Durability card (strongest over time, doesn't exist yet): the version-dated Basecamp Path.** A data moat — the tested, re-cut, version-dated sequence earned by watching where real beginners actually quit. Nearly impossible to copy once built (the copier didn't watch the failures that produced it), worthless as a launch claim (not yet earned). **Hard dependency: requires an instrumented drop-off feedback loop from day one** — a one-tap "where did you get stuck?" after each Path task, a per-task completion checkpoint, and a periodic micro-survey. Passive YouTube comments + email open-rates are insufficient and survivor-biased (the dropouts stay silent). Build this instrumentation into the product at launch or the moat never forms.
- **Present-tense asset:** your practitioner expertise (3 yrs AFA agency + product design + 3 yrs in AI), framed as *service not status* — the reason the curation is trustworthy — plus the clarity of the positioning itself.

*One-line: counter-position to get in, build the Path to stay in.*

## 5. North Star Metric

*What's the one number that, if it goes up, means everything else is working?*

> Good: one metric, written down, with a 90-day target — a leading indicator of revenue you know off the top of your head
> Bad: three north stars, vanity metrics (signups, downloads, followers), targets with no deadline

**Common north stars by business type:**

| Business type | Likely north star |
| --- | --- |
| Subscription SaaS | MRR |
| Mobile app | Trial-to-paid conversion rate |
| LTD / one-time | Weekly buyers |
| Consumer app w/ retention | Weekly active payers |
| Marketplace | GMV |
| Productized service | Active retainers |

**Your answer:** **Sequenced to match what each phase can actually move** (a north star you can't move isn't a north star, it's discouragement):

- **Phase 0 (pre-launch, no product): weekly *qualified* email subscribers.** The only output your current work can move. *Qualified* = **all three of:** (1) **source-matched** — arrived via reassurance/"feel-behind" beginner content, not a generic "AI tools" or make-money video; (2) **activated** — actually completed the Starter Kit's first task (a doer, not a free-PDF collector); (3) **self-identified** — chose "I feel behind and want to catch up" on a one-tap opt-in micro-survey (vs. "make money with AI" / "already use AI"). This phase's real job: build the audience *and* mine the voice-of-customer language and failure-order data that forge the Path moat.
- **Phase 1 (first product live): weekly buyers + subscriber→buyer conversion rate.** Switch the instant there's a $17 pack to sell. Watch **both**: raw buyers can stay low simply because the list is small, but the **conversion rate tells you whether the offer itself works**, independent of traffic size — the thing you most need to learn early. List growth demotes to the watched input.

The two hand off; the other is always visible as the key input. *Avoid:* permanent weekly-buyers (feels like failure for all of pre-launch) or permanent subscribers (the 50k-subscribers-no-revenue trap). `[set 90-day targets once channel cadence is known]`
