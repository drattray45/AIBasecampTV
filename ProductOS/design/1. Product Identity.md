# Product Identity Framework

> **Drafted: June 2026.** Built from `docs/PRODUCT.md` and reconciled with the existing Scout character bible + Pillar-1 assets. Brand: **AIBasecamp.TV** — beginner-AI education for everyday people who feel behind. Guide character: **Scout**, a warm brown bear with an always-on headlamp.

## Describe your product using your identity

> "We're a [archetype] brand on a mission to [mission]. Where most [category] brands believe [conventional belief], we believe [contrarian belief] — because [reasoning]. We sound [tone attributes] and the world we build feels [visual world]."

**We're an Everyman brand on a mission to help everyday people use AI for the real things in their life — and feel completely capable doing it. Where most AI education is built to keep you coming back, we're built to make ourselves unnecessary — because you were never behind, you were just handed AI in the wrong order. We sound warm-but-not-parental, plain-but-not-dumbed-down, calm and honest — and the world we build feels like a calm, warm, hand-drawn camp with Scout beside you: a headlamp glowing in the dark to get you here, warm daylight once you're learning. Never purple gradients.**

---

## 1. Mission

*What change are you making in the world for your customer so they can achieve their outcome? Pulls directly from the Customer and Outcome in your Product Offer.*

> Good: customer-anchored, ambitious, clearly worded, one sentence
> Bad: vague, internal-focused, slogan-y, about you instead of the customer

**Your answer:** Help everyday people use AI for the real things in their life — and feel completely capable doing it. *(Describes the world after Scout's done his job; the "feeling behind" wound lives in the hooks and first-video copy, never in the mission.)*

---

## 2. Contrarian Belief

*What does your category get wrong that you get right? Frame it as: "Most [category] brands believe [conventional wisdom], but we believe [opposite] — because [reasoning]."*

> Good: specific, defensible, provably true, names the opponent you're rejecting
> Bad: generic, untested, just-different-for-difference's-sake, no reasoning

**Your answer:** Most AI education is built to keep you coming back; we're built to make ourselves unnecessary — because you were never behind, you were just handed AI in the wrong order. Fix the order, and you don't need a guru: you need a good first step and a door out. *(Kept as pure worldview — the structural proof, the one-time ladder + capability-only guarantee + 60-day refund, lives in the offer copy and demonstrates the belief rather than stating it. This protects the creed even when a Phase-2 "stay current" membership arrives: graduating someone from beginner-fear isn't the same as never serving them again. The diagnosis — "you were never the problem, the order was" — is the load-bearing premise; without it, "we make ourselves unnecessary" reads as a gimmick instead of a moral stance.)*

---

## 3. Personality

*Which Jungian archetype is your brand? Pick one primary (and optionally one secondary).*

> Good: one primary archetype owned consistently, optionally paired with a complementary secondary
> Bad: trying to be all 12, picking the most flattering one with no evidence, mixing contradictory archetypes

**Your answer:** **Everyman** — and Everyman is the *whole* archetype, because an archetype is the *relationship the brand offers*, not the mascot's personality. The relationship is: *you're one of us, no pedestal, no priesthood* — the anti-condescension moat every AI guru violates. **Caregiver is not a second archetype; it's the warmth register Scout speaks in** to make the Everyman relationship feel safe. Logging it as "Caregiver-secondary" would license a future writer to make Scout *parental* — and parental is condescension wearing a sweater; a tenured 49-year-old ops manager would rather eat glass than be mothered through ChatGPT. **Sage is structurally forbidden** (not just avoided): the moment the brand claims to be the keeper of the proven order, it rebuilds the hierarchy the contrarian belief just tore down. The teacher stands *beside* her. *Scout scouts ahead on the trail (testing the tools so she doesn't flail — that's service), then walks the path beside her, never lecturing from in front.*

---

## 4. Tone of Voice

*How does the brand speak — what's the cadence, what words does it use, what words does it avoid?*

> Good: specific, distinctive, consistent across every surface
> Bad: "professional yet friendly", generic corporate-speak, vague, inconsistent surface-to-surface

**Your answer:**

**Voice attributes (each one excludes something):**
- **Warm, but not parental** — peer-with-your-back, never "let me help you, dear."
- **Plain, but not dumbed-down** — she's a tenured manager; the problem was sequence, not capacity.
- **Calm, but not boring** — the anti-hype, anti-FOMO register.
- **Encouraging, but not a cheerleader** — real "nice, you did that," never fake "you've got this!!!"
- **Honest, but not preachy** — the Patagonia directness.

**We say:** "you're not behind," "here's the first step," "let's try one you already know," "nice — you just did that," "no rush," "that wasn't your fault," "if you feel behind, you're exactly where you should be — at basecamp," "I tried the confusing way so you don't have to."

**We never say:** "unlock," "supercharge," "10x," "game-changer," "blow your mind," "don't get left behind," "everyone else is already ahead," "revolutionize" — **and the anti-condescension teeth:** never **"easy," "simple," "just"** (as in "just type this"), **"obviously,"** or **"for dummies."** Telling a scared beginner it's "easy" or to "just do X" is the cruelest word choice available: if it's so easy and she still can't, you've confirmed she's dumb. We never call the thing easy — we make it *doable.* *(Banned "just" applies to minimizing her task; it is fine nowhere in the canonical voice — keep the rule absolute.)*

**Example sentence (the tuning fork — read it aloud to check any copy):** *"That bland answer wasn't you failing — it was the wrong starting point, that's all. Let's try one you already know."*

## 5. Visual World

*What does the brand look like? Not a color palette — the mood, imagery, composition, and references that define the visual atmosphere.*

> Good: distinctive, considered, references named explicitly so the look can be reproduced by anyone on the team
> Bad: stock photos, "modern and clean", everyone-else's-mood-board, undefined visual rules

**Your answer:**

**Imagery style — Scout-led illustration, permanently; photography is earned-proof only.**
- A warm, characterful **hand-drawn system** (*New Yorker / Notion warmth, never Saturday-morning cartoon* — drawn for a 49-year-old, not her grandkid) carries ~90% of the visual load. **Scout** is the centre of it: a friendly brown bear, round open face, never fierce, with an **always-on headlamp** — the single most important brand asset, a glowing beacon that reads as AIBasecamp even cropped to a corner.
- **The real deliverable is a Scout *kit*, not a character bible:** an expression/pose/scene set mapped to actual content moments — *Scout-pointing-at-the-first-step, Scout-beside-her-(never-above), Scout-celebrating-a-small-win, Scout-at-the-door-out.* The difference between a mascot and a functioning visual language across thumbnails, in-video, lead magnet, course, and email.
- **Photography is earned-proof only:** the *only* photo that ever enters this system is a real, named, testimonial-willing student showing a result she chose to share — **no stock "real people," ever, pre- or post-launch** (stock is illustration with a worse aesthetic and none of Scout's ownability). It arrives at a named milestone — first testimonial-willing student — not at launch. Until then: Scout, full stop. *(Illustration also protects the anti-shame core: documentary photos of beginners "struggling" risk reading as "look at these confused people," and the watching Diane casts herself as an object of pity. Scout is a drawing she projects onto, not a peer she's invited to pity.)*

**Composition rules:**
- **Two modes.** *Marketing/thumbnails:* dramatic **teal mountain-night** (campfire-dark, NOT techy dark-mode) so the **headlamp beacon glows** — this is the feed-recognition asset. *Learning surfaces (course, Starter Kit, email, site):* **warm paper/daylight**, calm, generous negative space, high legibility — the place she studies should feel safe, not dramatic.
- **Calm, generous negative space** everywhere — the visual enactment of "we removed the firehose." One thing at a time, never a wall of options.
- **Large, legible type as a brand value, not an accessibility checkbox** — the reader is 49; small low-contrast grey text would betray the belief on the page. Readability *is* Everyman.
- **Colour:** warm brown Scout; teal-slate mountain grounds (marketing) / warm off-white (learning); **one yellow reserved for the headlamp, a single highlight word, and CTAs** — never decorative.
- **Type:** **Source Serif 4** for headlines (literary, refined editorial serif); **Hanken Grotesk** for body (friendly, highly legible at size). Deliberately *not* Inter / Outfit / Instrument Serif / Plus Jakarta — the vibe-coded AI defaults. *(Replaces the Arial placeholder in current assets.)*
- **Never in frame:** purple gradients, abstract AI orbs, 3D blobs, robots, glowing-neural anything, headset-stock, slick-UI-floating-in-space.

**Visual references (≥2 outside AI):** Headspace (calm, anxiety-lowering warmth); Patagonia / Airbnb (real-not-models documentary — the *earned* photography layer); Notion (illustration humanizing a tool); mid-century Penguin how-to (plainspoken instructional warmth).

## Visual Inspiration

*A starting library tailored to the identity above. Treat as a working set, not a final reference — replace items as the brand's visual world matures.*

### Brands to study
- **Duolingo** (app + duolingo.com) — how one character carries an entire product's visual load across app, push, and ads. *Take the mascot-kit discipline; leave the manic chaos.*
- **Mailchimp** (mailchimp.com) — Freddie + warm editorial illustration humanizing software, "smart not academic" voice. *Take warmth-over-corporate.*
- **Basecamp / 37signals** (basecamp.com + the books *Rework*, *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work*) — calm, anti-hustle, plainspoken confidence. *Take the anti-hype posture (and note the name-kinship).*
- **King Arthur Baking** (kingarthurbaking.com) — expert adult instruction that never once makes you feel dumb. *Take the teach-without-talking-down register.*
- **Trader Joe's** (the Fearless Flyer, in-store signage) — hand-drawn, plainspoken, unmistakably for regular people. *Take the everyman hand-drawn warmth and anti-slick confidence.*

### Where to browse
- It's Nice That (itsnicethat.com), The Brand Identity (the-brandidentity.com), Brand New (underconsideration.com/brandnew), Eye on Design (eyeondesign.aiga.org), Fonts In Use (fontsinuse.com).
- Are.na — search channels around *mascot systems, editorial illustration, warm web design.*
- Illustrators **Christoph Niemann** and **Jean Jullien** — warm, witty, simple line work.

### Type, photography, and film references
- **Headline type: Source Serif 4** (Google Fonts; literary, refined serif — calm authority, never techy).
- **Body type: Hanken Grotesk** (Google Fonts; distinctive, friendly, highly legible at large sizes). *(Both free; deliberately avoid Inter / Outfit / Instrument Serif / Plus Jakarta.)*
- **Photography north star (earned stage): Humans of New York** — real people, their own words, dignity *not* pity. The exact model for "a real student, shown because she wanted to share."
- **Tonal / film reference: Mister Rogers' Neighborhood** — the gold standard for speaking to someone who feels small with total respect and zero condescension. That's Scout's pacing.
