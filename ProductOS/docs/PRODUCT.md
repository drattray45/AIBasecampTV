# AIBasecamp.TV

*Beginner AI for people who feel behind.*

*Last updated: June 2026*

---

## Summary

AIBasecamp.TV helps non-technical adults who feel they've already missed the boat on AI go from "I closed the tab in frustration" to confidently using AI for five real, everyday tasks in their first week. Free YouTube videos — fronted by Scout, a friendly cartoon bear — draw people in; the website is the product, with a free Starter Kit, low-cost packs, and a beginner course. The channel is the marketing; the domain is the product. It's a creator-led education business, not an app — and it's built for the scared beginner the rest of the AI world talks over.

## Customer

Diane is a 49-year-old marketing or operations manager with 15+ years of earned competence — respected, capable, not clerical. The problem shows up on a Tuesday afternoon, just after a meeting where a colleague half her age said "oh, I just had ChatGPT do it" and everyone nodded, so she nodded too. Back at her desk she opens an incognito tab so no one sees her search history, types a vague question, gets a bland answer, and closes it. That night at 9pm at the kitchen island, kids settled, she finally googles "how to actually use ChatGPT" where it's safe to not know. Today she gets by on Google, abandoned YouTube tutorials, and a LinkedIn Learning course she started and never finished. Not for: the eager 28-year-old already automating with AI who wants advanced content, or the "make money with AI" hustle-seeker — both are the wrong fit and would drown out the nervous beginner.

## Problem

Diane's core wound is a quiet verdict on herself: *"I'm just not getting it."* She tried AI, it underwhelmed her, and she decided the problem was her — not her inputs. The trigger fires weekly and rising: a colleague demos AI in a meeting, a job posting in her field now lists "AI tools," her company announces an "AI initiative." The cost is low in cash but high in cortisol — wasted evenings and a creeping fear of becoming obsolete in a career she's not ready to leave. Every "just use AI" resource she finds assumes a starting point she doesn't have and begins at step three; worse, she can't tell whether an AI answer is right, so she won't trust it on anything that matters. And asking for help means admitting the gap to people whose respect is her professional capital — so she stays stuck, in silence.

## Mechanism

The reason free content fails Diane isn't that the information is missing — it's that it's infinite, unsequenced, and pitched over her head, which is what made her quit. AIBasecamp's answer is the Basecamp Path: the *proven order* to learn the five things that matter, built on one rule — confidence before capability. Task one is rigged to manufacture an effortless win on home turf (she asks the AI about something she already knows, and feels it "get" her) before any demand is placed on her; the flashy, image-style stuff is deliberately saved for last so it never overwhelms her early. Along the way she gets exact copy-paste prompts that fix the precise wall she hit ("the answer was bland because nobody showed you what to type"), guided by Scout — a cartoon bear who literally can't look down on her, which removes the shame that makes a 50-year-old stop asking questions. The order itself is the product; the warmth makes it survivable.

## Why

Two advantages, by time horizon. To get in the door: counter-positioning — a calm, jargon-free, "you're-not-behind" brand in a niche full of hype and complexity, a lane the technical AI creators and hustle-sellers can't occupy without alienating their own audiences. To stay hard to beat: the version-dated Basecamp Path, refined from watching where real beginners actually quit — a sequence almost impossible to copy because the copier didn't witness the failures that produced it (it requires drop-off feedback built in from day one). The risk it takes off Diane's table: a 60-day, no-questions money-back promise framed as "do the five tasks, and if it still doesn't click, that's on us to teach better — not on you to try harder." She never has to prove she did the work, and she never needs a paid AI subscription to finish.

## Business model

One-time digital products — no subscription at launch, because Diane's psychology wants to buy once and be done (a recurring charge is a monthly re-decision and a visible statement line that reintroduces her shame). The ladder: a free Starter Kit (the lead magnet) → a $17 "5 AI Tasks" pack whose job is to turn a subscriber into a first-time buyer → a flagship course at $99 founding, stepping to $149 then $199, with a payment plan at the top — all deliberately under Diane's $299 spending ceiling. Because there's no per-user AI cost, margins run ~85–90%; the real cost is front-loaded video production, so the binding constraint is distribution, not unit economics. A membership is a deliberate Phase-2 move, once the pitch can shift from "learn the basics" to "stay current." At $99–199 one-time, the course sits below a year of LinkedIn Learning ($240), Coursera ($399), or MasterClass ($120–240) — a finishable, owned purchase.

## Proof

The market is real: Heather Murray's "AI for Non-Techies" is a live, direct competitor (jargon-free AI training for non-technical people, a newsletter that grew into a course, membership, and free academy), alongside Scott Galloway's Section (~$600 crash course) and Zain Kahn's Superhuman AI (a free newsletter to over a million readers earning seven figures a year within four months). Tellingly, all of them lean B2B, career, or marketing-pro — the personal, home, reassurance-led "feel behind" buyer is served only at the edges, leaving a real lane open. Proof of *this* product specifically doesn't exist yet — it's pre-launch with zero student results, and that's the honest gating gap; the only present-tense credibility is the founder's three years running an AI-focused agency, framed as service ("I've made every mistake so you don't have to"). What's being tested next, in named Facebook and LinkedIn channels: a $17 Buy Button test (pass = 3+ cold-stranger card swipes and a 2–3% buy rate within 14 days or 300 visitors) running alongside a $99 founding "concierge" offer (pass = 2–3 finished transformations with a testimonial in 21 days). If Diane engages but doesn't buy while freelancers and small-business owners do, the plan pivots to that audience.

## Goal

The one number to watch changes with the stage. Before the product exists: weekly *qualified* email subscribers — people who came in through "feel behind" content, actually completed the Starter Kit's first task, and self-identified as beginners (not raw signups). The moment the first $17 pack is live: weekly buyers, watched together with the subscriber-to-buyer conversion rate, because the rate tells you the offer works even while traffic is still small. Ninety-day targets get set once the channel's posting cadence is known. What would make us change course: real traffic to the pack but near-zero cold swipes from Diane while a different audience bites — the signal to pivot who we're selling to.
