# BONUS - Product Identity Deep Dive

*A bonus asset for The Product Studio — a section-by-section breakdown of the Product Identity Framework with good and bad examples pulled from teardowns of category-defining brands: Patagonia, Tesla, Airbnb, Apple, Linear, Notion, Stripe, Liquid Death, Mailchimp, Slack, Headspace, Disney, Nike, and more. Sources include Margaret Mark and Carol Pearson's "The Hero and the Outlaw," Simon Sinek's Golden Circle, April Dunford's positioning framework, Adam Morgan's "Eating the Big Fish," and the Nielsen Norman Group's four-dimensions-of-tone research.*

---

## How to Use This Guide

The Product Identity Framework asks five questions. Most solo builders get stuck on each one because the question is short but the answer is hard. This guide walks through each section in detail, shows you what a strong answer looks like next to what a weak answer looks like, and gives you the mental model to spot the difference in your own work.

Read each section once before you fill in the framework. Then fill in the framework with a draft. Then come back to this guide and pressure-test each answer against the Good/Bad patterns. Three passes is the right cadence.

## 1. Mission

### Why This Matters

The mission is the sentence the rest of the brand is built on. If it's wrong, every downstream decision — tone, visuals, copy, ads, hiring, pricing — drifts. If it's right, the team and the customer pull in the same direction without being asked.

Simon Sinek's Golden Circle is the cleanest distinction: **Why** is your purpose (why you exist), **Vision** is the future world if your Why came true, and **Mission** is what you do daily to get there. Most founders confuse the three. The mission is the *active verb sentence* — what you do, for whom, with what result. Not "to be the leading…" but "to do X for Y so that Z."

### How to Write It

The strongest test: read your mission out loud to a stranger in your ICP. If they nod and say "yeah I want that," it's working. If they say "what does that actually mean?", you've drifted into corporate-speak.

A great mission has four properties:

- **Customer-anchored** — names a real person, not "users" or "the world."
- **Outcome-specific** — the verb describes a real change in the customer's life.
- **Ambitious but credible** — big enough to inspire, plausible enough not to embarrass.
- **One sentence** — if it takes a paragraph, you're missing the core idea.

### Good Examples

| Brand | Mission | Why it works |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Patagonia | "We're in business to save our home planet." | One sentence. Customer = everyone. Verb = save. Stakes = the planet. Couldn't be more clear. |
| Tesla | "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy." | Active verb (accelerate). Specific change (transition). Measurable scope (world's). Reframes Tesla from "car company" to "energy company." |
| Airbnb | "Create a world where anyone can belong anywhere." | Customer = anyone. Outcome = belonging. Anti-incumbent (hotels = anonymous; Airbnb = belonging). |
| Slack | "Make work life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive." | Customer = working person. Three concrete outcomes. Plain English. |
| LinkedIn | "Connect the world's professionals to make them more productive and successful." | Customer = professionals. Verb = connect. Outcome = productive + successful. |

### Bad Examples (and why)

- **"To be the world's leading platform for innovative solutions."** Vacuous on every axis. "World's leading" is internal vanity. "Innovative solutions" describes nothing. Could be a defense contractor or a candy company.
- **"To revolutionize the way people experience X."** "Revolutionize" is the most overused verb in the founder lexicon. Try replacing it with the actual change and watch the sentence either crystallize or fall apart.
- **"Empowering businesses to achieve their full potential."** Who is the customer? What's the outcome? What does "full potential" mean? Three layers of abstraction stacked.
- **"Our mission is excellence in everything we do."** Excellence isn't a mission, it's a brag.

### Good vs Bad — Side-by-Side

| Bad (vague) | Good (specific) |
| --- | --- |
| "To empower creators" | "Create a world where anyone can belong anywhere" (Airbnb) |
| "To revolutionize mobility" | "Accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy" (Tesla) |
| "To deliver innovative tools for teams" | "Make work life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive" (Slack) |
| "To be the trusted partner for businesses" | "We're in business to save our home planet" (Patagonia) |

## 2. Contrarian Belief

### Why This Matters

Brands that look just like their category get treated like the category. The contrarian belief is the *wedge* — the deliberately unfashionable position you take that makes your brand legible against the rest of the market.

April Dunford's positioning framework names five components, but the one that decides the rest is **differentiated value**: "what we can do for a customer that no other alternative can." The contrarian belief is the *narrative version* of differentiated value. It's the sentence that explains why the category is wrong and you are right.

Adam Morgan's "Eating the Big Fish" introduced the challenger-brand model 25+ years ago, and it has only become more important: in a category with a dominant incumbent, the only viable wedge for a new entrant is *taking a position the incumbent cannot copy.*

### How to Write It

The structure is: **"Most [category] brands believe [conventional wisdom], but we believe [opposite] — because [reasoning]."**

The reasoning is the load-bearing piece. Anyone can say "we're different." The contrarian belief is *defensibly* different — backed by a customer truth, a technology shift, or a category insight the incumbents have ignored.

Three tests for a strong contrarian belief:

- **Is there a named opponent?** Vague disagreement is weak. "Unlike X, we believe Y" is strong.
- **Is it provably true?** If a customer disagrees with the premise, the belief collapses. Test it against 5 real customers.
- **Does it cost you something?** Real contrarian beliefs exclude some customers. If your belief offends nobody, it's not contrarian — it's marketing copy.

### Good Examples

| Brand | Contrarian belief | Why it works |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Liquid Death | "Most water brands believe water should signal life and wellness — we believe water should be punk, irreverent, and metal — because health products treat their customers like children." | Names the opponent. Defies category convention. Has a customer-truth root. Costs them the wellness segment. |
| Tesla | "Most car brands believe electric cars are an eco-compromise — we believe electric is the *better* car — because batteries, software, and torque are the future of automotive performance." | Reframes EVs from "save the planet" (small market) to "go faster" (big market). |
| Linear | "Most project management tools believe more configuration = more value — we believe opinion is the product — because configuration debt slows teams down more than it empowers them." | Defies Jira/Asana category orthodoxy. Names the cost (config debt). Anchors in customer experience. |
| Notion | "Most productivity tools believe each tool should do one thing well — we believe one tool should do everything — because tool-switching is the actual productivity tax." | Inverts the "best of breed" SaaS dogma. Defensible in customer behavior. |
| Cursor | "Most AI coding tools believe AI assists the developer — we believe AI writes the code and the developer reviews — because the productivity gain from writing-mode is 10x assist-mode." | Reframes the human-AI relationship. Anchored in measurable outcomes. |
| Mailchimp | "Most B2B brands believe business buyers are rational — we believe B2B is emotional too — because real people make real decisions, even at work." | Defies B2B-tone-of-voice orthodoxy. Justifies the playful brand voice. |
| Patagonia | "Most outdoor brands believe more sales = more success — we believe you should buy less, and what you buy should last forever — because the planet can't sustain endless consumption." | Sacrifices revenue for principle. Visibly costs Patagonia growth — which is the proof. |

### Bad Examples (and why)

- **"We believe in putting the customer first."** Not contrarian. Every brand says this.
- **"We're different because we care more."** No opponent named. No reasoning. Just an assertion.
- **"Unlike legacy tools, we're modern."** Modern vs. legacy is a positioning *style*, not a contrarian belief. What does "modern" mean? Why is it better?
- **"We believe AI should be ethical."** Universally agreeable — therefore says nothing.
- **"We're like Stripe but for X."** That's a category pitch, not a contrarian belief. The contrarian belief is *why* the X version is different from Stripe.

### Good vs Bad — Side-by-Side

| Bad (universal) | Good (defensible) |
| --- | --- |
| "We believe in great design" | "We believe opinion is the product" (Linear — names a tradeoff competitors won't make) |
| "We believe AI should help humans" | "We believe AI writes the code, the developer reviews" (Cursor — picks a specific position) |
| "We believe in transparency" | "We believe you should buy less" (Patagonia — costs them revenue, proving sincerity) |
| "We believe water should be healthier" | "We believe water should be punk" (Liquid Death — defies category) |

## 3. Personality (Jungian Archetype)

### Why This Matters

Without a personality, your brand is competing on price and features. With one, your brand has a *recognizable voice* the customer can identify before they read the logo. Margaret Mark and Carol Pearson's "The Hero and the Outlaw" (2001) adapted Jung's 12 archetypes to brands, and 25+ years later it's still the most useful framework solo founders can use. It works because human brains pattern-match characters — we can describe Tesla in one word (Magician), Patagonia in one word (Explorer), Nike in one word (Hero), and Liquid Death in one word (Outlaw), and the descriptions are *transmissible* to anyone else.

The 12 archetypes split into four cardinal orientations:

- **Provide structure** (Ruler, Caregiver, Creator) — make the world more ordered.
- **Pursue a journey** (Innocent, Explorer, Sage) — seek paradise / understanding.
- **Leave a legacy** (Hero, Outlaw, Magician) — make an impact / change the world.
- **Connect with others** (Lover, Jester, Everyman) — belong / relate.

### How to Pick One

You're picking *one primary*. Optionally one *secondary* that supports it. Never three. Never all twelve. The strongest brands in the world commit to one archetype and own it for decades.

Three tests:

- **Does it match your contrarian belief?** A Caregiver doesn't say "buy less and break the rules." An Outlaw doesn't say "let us hold your hand."
- **Does it match your customer's self-image?** Nike's customers want to *be* the Hero. Patagonia's customers want to *be* the Explorer. Apple's customers want to *be* the Magician (or feel like one).
- **Can you live with it for a decade?** Brand personalities work because they're consistent over years. If the archetype feels limiting after a week, pick a different one.

### The 12 Archetypes — Deep Reference

| Archetype | Motivation | Core desire | Tone | Brand examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Innocent** | Be happy. Restore faith. | Get to paradise. | Optimistic, simple, pure, sincere. | Coca-Cola, Dove, Tom's of Maine, McDonald's (early). |
| **Sage** | Understand the world. | Find the truth. | Wise, expert, calm, authoritative. | Google, BBC, The Economist, MIT, Bloomberg. |
| **Explorer** | Find yourself by discovering the world. | Freedom. | Adventurous, independent, restless, pioneering. | Patagonia, Jeep, Red Bull, REI, NatGeo. |
| **Outlaw** | Disrupt what isn't working. | Revolution. | Rebellious, defiant, punk, irreverent. | Harley-Davidson, Virgin, Liquid Death, Diesel. |
| **Magician** | Make dreams come true. | Transform reality. | Visionary, mystical, "make-the-impossible-real." | Apple, Disney, Tesla, Polaroid, MAC Cosmetics. |
| **Hero** | Prove your worth through action. | Master a challenge. | Courageous, determined, brave, inspiring. | Nike, FedEx, Adidas, Duracell, US Army. |
| **Lover** | Achieve intimacy. | Be desired and desire others. | Passionate, sensual, intimate, refined. | Chanel, Victoria's Secret, Häagen-Dazs, Godiva. |
| **Jester** | Live in the moment. | Joy. | Playful, irreverent, fun, witty. | Old Spice, Skittles, M&Ms, Aviation Gin. |
| **Everyman** | Belong with others. | Connect. | Relatable, honest, down-to-earth, friendly. | IKEA, Target, Levi's, Home Depot, Costco. |
| **Caregiver** | Protect and care for others. | Help others. | Nurturing, generous, compassionate, warm. | Johnson & Johnson, Volvo, UNICEF, Dove (also Innocent), Campbell's. |
| **Ruler** | Exert control. | Prosperity and order. | Authoritative, premium, in-control, refined. | Rolex, Mercedes-Benz, Microsoft, American Express, Hugo Boss. |
| **Creator** | Build something of enduring value. | Imagination realized. | Imaginative, expressive, self-expressive, bold. | Lego, Adobe, Crayola, Lululemon (also Lover), Apple (also Magician). |

### Good Examples

- **Nike — Hero.** Every Nike ad is a Hero arc: ordinary person → struggle → triumph. "Just Do It" is the most efficient Hero tagline ever written. They have never wavered.
- **Apple — Magician (with Creator secondary).** "Think Different." Product launches feel like reveals. The visual identity is mysterious and powerful. The competitor is the impossible, not the other PCs.
- **Patagonia — Explorer.** Documentary photography of real conditions. Mission about the planet. Customers are climbers, surfers, skiers — actual explorers.
- **Liquid Death — Outlaw.** Heavy-metal aesthetic. Skull-and-crossbones. "Murder your thirst." Defies every convention of the bottled-water category.
- **Disney — Magician.** "Where dreams come true." Every product launch is a transformation moment. The opening castle logo is literally a magic transformation.

### Bad Examples (and why)

- **A startup picks "Hero" because it sounds heroic — but their product is a budgeting app.** Mismatch between archetype and customer relationship. A budgeting app is a Caregiver (helps you take care of yourself) or a Sage (teaches you), not a Hero (overcomes a struggle).
- **A B2B SaaS tries to be the Outlaw without taking real risk.** "We're disrupting the industry" while shipping the same enterprise sales motion as everyone else. Outlaw requires actual rule-breaking, not just rule-claiming.
- **Gap's drift from Everyman to Rebel.** Documented case: Gap repositioned as Rebel while maintaining conservative campaigns, lost brand loyalty and sales. The archetype-action mismatch was visible.
- **Mixing Caregiver + Outlaw.** Cannot be both nurturing and disruptive at the same time. Pick one or you confuse the audience.
- **Mixing Ruler + Jester.** Cannot be both authoritative and irreverent at the same time. Old Spice tried this and the Old Spice Guy (Jester) only worked once they fully committed.

### Good vs Bad — Side-by-Side

| Bad (mismatched / mixed) | Good (consistent) |
| --- | --- |
| Hero archetype + customer-success-focused product | Caregiver archetype + customer-success-focused product |
| Outlaw archetype + enterprise sales motion | Outlaw archetype + community-led growth motion (Linear, Liquid Death) |
| Magician + Caregiver in same brand | Magician primary + Creator secondary (Apple) |
| Picking 4 archetypes "to be flexible" | Picking 1 primary + (optional) 1 secondary |

## 4. Tone of Voice

### Why This Matters

The personality is *who the brand is*. The tone of voice is *how it sounds when it speaks.* The Nielsen Norman Group's research is the cleanest framework for thinking about this: every brand's voice can be located on four dimensions.

- **Funny ↔ Serious** — humor, playfulness, lightheartedness vs. somber, businesslike.
- **Formal ↔ Casual** — professional structure vs. familiar, conversational.
- **Respectful ↔ Irreverent** — polite, deferential vs. quirky, edgy.
- **Enthusiastic ↔ Matter-of-Fact** — high-energy, emotive vs. calm, neutral.

A great brand voice is **consistent across every surface** (website, app, push notification, error message, support reply, ad copy) and **distinctive enough that a stranger could identify the brand** with the logo removed.

### How to Write It

Three concrete deliverables for a solo builder. If you can do these three, you have a brand voice. If you can't, you have an aspiration.

#### 4a. Voice attributes (3–5 adjectives)

Pick 3–5 adjectives that describe *how the brand sounds*. Generic words ("professional," "friendly," "helpful") are the enemy. Reach for specific words that exclude something.

- **Bad:** "Professional, friendly, helpful, innovative, customer-focused."
- **Good (Mailchimp):** "Smart, but not academic. Authentic, but not stuffy. Helpful, but not bossy."
- **Good (Slack):** "Confident, direct, human."
- **Good (Linear):** "Precise, calm, opinionated."
- **Good (Headspace):** "Calm, empathetic, never preachy."

The Mailchimp pattern — *"X, but not Y"* — is the single most useful tone-of-voice convention in the entire field. It forces you to define what you're not, which makes what you are concrete.

#### 4b. We say / we don't say

A list of specific words and phrases the brand uses, paired with explicit no-go words.

- **Good (Slack):** Avoid jargon and fluff. Avoid being insensitive or too witty. Even when playful, keep it clean.
- **Good (Mailchimp):** Active voice, not passive. Plain English, not slang. Positive language, not negative.
- **Good for a startup:** "We say *ship*, never *deploy*. We say *folks*, never *guys*. We say *teammates*, never *resources*. We say *let's go*, never *unlock*."

The point is **specificity**. "Avoid jargon" is too vague to follow. "Never say *deploy*, always say *ship*" is followable.

#### 4c. Example sentence

One sentence that nobody could mistake for another brand.

- **Good (Linear):** "We rebuilt notifications. They're faster now."
- **Good (Patagonia):** "Don't buy this jacket."
- **Good (Liquid Death):** "Murder your thirst."
- **Good (Nike):** "Just do it."
- **Good (Apple):** "Think different."

You should be able to read your sentence to someone in your category and have them say "that sounds like you."

### Good Examples

| Brand | Voice attributes | Example sentence | Why it works |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Linear | Precise, calm, opinionated. | "We rebuilt notifications. They're faster now." | Confident. Minimal. Developer-respecting. |
| Mailchimp | Smart-not-academic. Authentic-not-stuffy. Helpful-not-bossy. | "Now you can talk to your customers like real people." | Conversational. Excludes corporate-speak. |
| Slack | Confident, direct, human. | "It's all about you. You drive Slack." | Clarity over cleverness. Talks like a person. |
| Headspace | Calm, empathetic, never preachy. | "Take a deep breath. Just one." | Reduces the user's stress, doesn't add to it. |
| Patagonia | Direct, principled, unsentimental. | "Don't buy this jacket." | Defies category. Tone matches contrarian belief. |
| Liquid Death | Punk, irreverent, deadpan. | "Murder your thirst." | Wholly committed to the Outlaw archetype. |
| Apple | Confident, minimal, declarative. | "It's the most personal iPhone ever." | Confidence is the whole brand. |

### Bad Examples (and why)

- **"Professional yet friendly."** The most common tone-of-voice anti-pattern. Tells you nothing. Means nothing. Excludes nothing.
- **"We're approachable but also experts."** Same. Trying to be everything, ending up being nothing.
- **Corporate-speak: "We leverage best-in-class solutions to deliver value to stakeholders."** Could be any B2B company. Use plain English.
- **Inconsistent tone across surfaces.** Marketing site is playful, app copy is formal, push notifications are casual. The user can't form a stable impression.
- **"Funny" without commitment.** Trying to be witty in some surfaces but defaulting to corporate in others. As Mailchimp warns: "Forced humor can be worse than none at all."

### Good vs Bad — Side-by-Side

| Bad (vague) | Good (specific) |
| --- | --- |
| "Professional yet friendly" | "Smart, but not academic. Authentic, but not stuffy." (Mailchimp) |
| "We're approachable" | "Confident, direct, human." (Slack) |
| "Helpful and innovative" | "Precise, calm, opinionated." (Linear) |
| "Empathetic to our users" | "Calm, empathetic, never preachy." (Headspace) |

## 5. Visual World

### Why This Matters

The visual world is what your brand *looks and feels like* before anyone reads a word. It's not a color palette. It's not your logo. It's the entire aesthetic atmosphere — imagery, composition, references, mood — that distinguishes your brand on a glance.

Most solo founders stop at color and font. The brands that win define a *world*: a coherent aesthetic universe that's reproducible by anyone on the team without needing the founder in the room.

### How to Define It

Three deliverables, in order.

#### 5a. Imagery style

How do you make pictures? Concretely:

- **Photography or illustration or 3D or abstract?** Pick one primary. (Some brands run two — Stripe pairs photography of customers with custom illustration for product. Most don't.)
- **What's the specific style?** "Photography" is not enough. "Shot on film, available light, real people, unposed" is enough. "Illustration" is not enough. "Custom hand-drawn line illustrations with limited palette" is enough.
- **What's *not* in the image?** Often more important than what is. Patagonia's photography never features models. Apple's product shots never feature people using the product in the hero image. Linear's marketing site has no people at all.

#### 5b. Composition rules

How is content arranged on the page?

- **Asymmetric vs. symmetric grids?**
- **Negative space — generous or dense?**
- **Type-led or image-led?**
- **Centered or off-axis?**

A brand with documented composition rules is reproducible by anyone. A brand without them looks different on every page.

#### 5c. Visual references

The 3–5 named brands, films, artists, or aesthetic worlds the brand draws from. References are the load-bearing piece — they make the visual world *transmissible.* "We want it to feel modern" is not a reference. "We want it to feel like *Wes Anderson directing a Patagonia campaign*" is a reference.

### Good Examples

| Brand | Imagery style | Composition | References | Why it works |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Apple | Minimalist product photography on white. Glass/metal materials. | Centered, generous negative space, single-product hero. | Bauhaus, Dieter Rams, mid-century industrial design. | Consistency across 40 years. Materially distinct from every competitor. |
| Patagonia | Documentary outdoor photography. Real climbers/surfers/skiers in real conditions. | Wide landscape framing, environment as subject. | National Geographic, Yvon Chouinard's own climbing photography, Galen Rowell. | Photography is the proof of the contrarian belief. |
| Linear | Pure UI screenshots. Mostly product surfaces. Minimal use of people. | Single-product focus, dark mode, asymmetric grids, generous negative space. | Apple, Stripe, Things 3, Notion. | Calm, confident, developer-respecting. Matches voice and archetype. |
| Liquid Death | Heavy-metal illustration. Horror movie aesthetics. Death imagery. | Centered, bold, posters-as-design. | Heavy-metal album covers, horror movie posters, B-movie aesthetics, MAD Magazine. | The visual world is the entire brand. Every surface is the Outlaw archetype. |
| Airbnb | Natural lifestyle photography of real travelers in real homes. Warm, candid moments. | Bright, daylight, slice-of-life. | Travel journalism (NYT Travel), documentary photography, Annie Leibovitz casual portraiture. | The photography is the product proof — "real homes, real people." |
| Stripe | Custom illustrations of technical concepts. Photography of customer founders. | Dense type-led layouts with generous illustration islands. | Swiss design, the Whole Earth Catalog, Domus magazine, technical drawings. | Illustration humanizes a deeply technical product. |
| Notion | Hand-drawn illustration. Marker-style icons. Personality through doodles. | Page-as-canvas, blocks, asymmetric. | Children's book illustration, sketch notebooks, Penguin paperback covers. | The illustration system signals "this tool is for thinking." |

### Bad Examples (and why)

- **Stock photography.** Generic businesswomen with headsets. Smiling diverse teams pointing at laptops. These say "I had no time / budget / vision." Use anything else — even bad custom photography is more brand-positive than great stock.
- **"Modern and clean."** Means nothing. Every B2B SaaS marketing site in 2026 says this. If it doesn't tell a designer what to do, it's not a brief.
- **The everyone-else mood board.** Pinterest scrape of all current trends. Result: looks like everyone else in your category. The point of references is to draw from *outside* your category — that's where distinctiveness comes from.
- **Inconsistent imagery across surfaces.** Marketing site uses photography, app uses illustration, social uses 3D. The visual world fragments. Pick a primary style and let secondaries support it.
- **Undocumented rules.** "I just know it when I see it." Works when there's only the founder. Falls apart when anyone else is making decisions. **The visual world has to be teachable.**

### Good vs Bad — Side-by-Side

| Bad (generic) | Good (specific) |
| --- | --- |
| "Stock photography of teams collaborating" | "Documentary photography of real climbers, never models" (Patagonia) |
| "Modern and clean" | "Centered single-product on white, generous negative space, mid-century industrial references" (Apple) |
| Pinterest mood board of current trends | "Heavy-metal album covers, horror movie posters, MAD Magazine" (Liquid Death) |
| "Illustrated style" | "Hand-drawn line illustrations, Penguin paperback covers, children's-book references" (Notion) |
| Founder-only judgment ("I'll know it when I see it") | Documented composition rules + 3–5 named references anyone on the team can apply |

## How the Five Sections Compose

The five sections of the Product Identity Framework are not independent. They have to *agree.* The single most common failure mode in solo-builder brand work is sections that contradict each other — an Outlaw archetype with a Caregiver tone of voice, or a "save the planet" mission with stock photography.

The test: read all five answers aloud in sequence. They should sound like the same brand.

- **Mission** → tells you what the brand is doing in the world.
- **Contrarian belief** → tells you why the brand exists and what it rejects.
- **Personality** → tells you what character the brand is playing.
- **Tone of voice** → tells you how that character speaks.
- **Visual world** → tells you what that character looks like.

If a customer can read all five and describe the brand back to you in one sentence, you have a working identity. If they can't, the five sections aren't pulling in the same direction yet.

---

## Closing — The One Mental Model That Beats Everything

> **A great brand identity is a character your customer can imagine in a room. You should be able to describe your brand as if it were a person — what they believe, how they talk, what they look like, what they would never do — and a stranger should be able to recognize them from the description alone. If you cannot pass that test, the identity is not yet finished. If you can, the five framework sections are doing their job.**

The contrarian belief tells you what they reject. The archetype tells you who they are. The tone tells you how they speak. The visual world tells you what they look like. The mission tells you what they're trying to do in the world. Five sections, one character, one brand.

That is how Patagonia, Apple, Linear, Notion, Liquid Death, Stripe, and every other category-defining brand built recognition — not by polishing one section but by making all five agree, in writing, on day one.
