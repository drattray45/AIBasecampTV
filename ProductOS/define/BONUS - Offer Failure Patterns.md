# BONUS - Offer Failure Patterns

Eight named patterns that explain why most first-draft product offers underperform. Each pattern has a diagnostic question, a "bad" version, an explanation of why it breaks downstream, and a "good" version. Use this document as a checklist after drafting your Product Offer — learning the patterns is how you stop creating them.

---

## 1. The Wide Wedge

The customer line names a category rather than a recognizable person.

**Diagnostic question:** Who would *not* buy this? If you can't name an excluded group, the wedge is too wide.

**Bad version:**

> Small business owners who need better tools.

**Why it breaks:** Ad spend gets burned on people who'll never convert. Onboarding has to be generic, so it satisfies nobody. Refund rates climb because the wrong people signed up. Pricing has to flex to cover too many willingness-to-pay tiers, so it ends up wrong for everyone. This is what hobbled Bench's early growth — broad enough to feel inclusive, narrow enough that nobody felt directly addressed.

**Good version (Linear):**

> Engineering and product teams at fast-growing software startups (10–500 people) who feel slowed down by Jira's configuration sprawl but need more workflow rigor than Trello or Notion can provide.

Notice what Linear's line names: a role, a company stage, a size range, and the two competing tools the customer has already tried. Anyone outside that frame self-excludes — which is the point.

---

## 2. The Vague Pain

The pain line describes a feeling, not an event.

**Diagnostic question:** Can the pain be reproduced in a screenshot or a one-paragraph story? If not, it's not specific enough to design against.

**Bad version:**

> Inefficient workflows that waste time and create frustration.

**Why it breaks:** Vague pain produces vague positioning, which produces vague copy, which gets ignored. Buyers don't act on "frustration"; they act on a moment they remember. Without a nameable moment, the offer has no trigger to fire on, and ad campaigns never find the buyer at the moment of buying intent.

**Good version (Bricks Builder):**

> WordPress freelancers shipping client sites that score under 60 on PageSpeed because Elementor and Divi stack shortcode soup that bloats page load past 3 seconds.

The pain is now reproducible: open the buyer's site, run PageSpeed, get a number. It can be measured before and after. That's the bar.

---

## 3. The Unmeasurable Outcome

The outcome line is a benefit, not a result.

**Diagnostic question:** 30 days in, would the buyer be able to tell whether they got the outcome? If not, the outcome is unfalsifiable — and an unfalsifiable outcome makes a meaningless guarantee.

**Bad version:**

> Better productivity and improved quality for your team.

**Why it breaks:** When the outcome can't be measured, the buyer can't tell if the product worked, so they churn on vibes. Refund requests become unwinnable arguments — there's no objective standard either side can point to. The guarantee section also collapses, because there's nothing concrete to guarantee against.

**Good version (Bricks Builder):**

> Build any custom WordPress site visually — including headers, footers, dynamic templates, and WooCommerce stores — that scores 95+ on PageSpeed Insights out of the box, with hand-clean markup instead of nested shortcode soup.

A buyer can verify "95+ on PageSpeed" in 60 seconds. That single number does the work of the entire outcome line.

---

## 4. The Feature-List Mechanism

The mechanism line is marketing chrome instead of an explanation of how the product works.

**Diagnostic question:** Does the mechanism name one thing about how the product works that competitors can't easily copy? If not, it's chrome.

**Bad version:**

> AI-powered, cloud-native platform with real-time collaboration and seamless integrations.

**Why it breaks:** Every competitor in the category can claim the same thing. Mechanism is where the offer earns its right to charge more than the cheapest credible alternative — when it dissolves into buzzwords, the price ceiling collapses with it.

**Good version (Linear):**

> A keyboard-first, opinionated workflow engine built on a custom real-time sync layer. Sub-50ms loads, deep Git/Slack/Figma integrations, and curated defaults remove the configuration debt that bogs down Jira deployments — the opinion is the product.

Linear's mechanism names a specific technical bet (a custom sync layer), a measurable consequence (sub-50ms loads), and a strategic posture (opinion as feature). A competitor can't copy that in a sprint, and the buyer can tell.

---

## 5. The Toothless Guarantee

The guarantee doesn't cost the seller real money if the outcome fails.

**Diagnostic question:** Does the guarantee actually reduce buyer risk in a way the seller would feel? If not, it doesn't move the deal.

**Bad version:**

> Satisfaction guaranteed — we're confident you'll love it.

**Why it breaks:** A guarantee is a risk transfer. If the seller doesn't bear any risk, the buyer doesn't get relief, and the line does no work in the offer. Buyers read past it. Sales objections that the guarantee was supposed to defuse stay alive.

**Good version (Linear):**

> Free for unlimited members on the Standard plan with unlimited issues, projects, and current-cycle scope — no credit card, no time-limited trial. Cancel paid plans in one click and keep your read-only data.

Linear's guarantee costs them real money — every free team is bandwidth and database load — and it removes every common SaaS objection at once: trial expiry pressure, credit-card lock-in, data hostage on cancellation. The buyer feels safer because the seller is bearing real cost.

## 6. The Hypothetical Proof

The proof line names belief instead of artifact.

**Diagnostic question:** Can you point to a public artifact — a review count, a named customer, a podcast mention, a revenue screenshot? If not, the proof is a hope.

**Bad version:**

> Customers love the product and tell us it transforms their workflow.

**Why it breaks:** Skeptical buyers — the ones whose objections actually drive your conversion rate — discount anything the seller says about themselves. Without external artifacts, the offer can only convert buyers who were already going to buy. The marginal customer, the one the offer was supposed to win over, walks away.

**Good version (Designjoy):**

> $1.5M+ ARR run solo, with monthly Stripe MRR screenshots posted publicly on X to 100K+ followers. Client work and testimonials from Castmagic, Tally, OpenPhone, and dozens of Product Hunt #1 launches; routinely cited as the canonical productized-service case study.

Every claim is independently verifiable by the buyer in under five minutes — the Stripe screenshots, the named clients, the Product Hunt rankings. That's what proof looks like when it's doing real work.

---

## 7. The Story Break

Each section reads fine in isolation, but together they describe three different products.

**Diagnostic question:** Read the assembled one-sentence pitch out loud — *"For \[customer\] to help them avoid \[pain\] and achieve \[outcome\]. \[Product\] does this using \[mechanism\], backed by \[proof\] and de-risking with \[guarantee\]."* Does it sound like one product, or like a Mad Libs page?

**Bad version:**

> *Customer:* Enterprise CIOs at Fortune 500 companies.
> *Outcome:* Save individual designers two hours a week.
> *Proof:* 4.7 stars on the Mac App Store.

**Why it breaks:** The customer named (enterprise CIO) does not care about the outcome named (saving designer hours) and is not moved by the proof named (consumer app store ratings). Each line was written from a different draft of the product, in a different week, and never reconciled. Almost every weak offer fails this test, and it's the cheapest one to fix — but only if you read the assembled sentence out loud.

**Good version (CleanShot X)**:The customer (Mac-using designers, devs, support reps, creators), the outcome (polished capture in under 10 seconds), the mechanism (pure native Swift/AppKit), the guarantee (14-day refund, lifetime updates), and the proof (4.9/5 across 3,000+ App Store ratings, Wirecutter, Setapp Editor's Choice) all describe the same person and the same buying context. One person, one story.

---

## 8. The Frame Mismatch

The offer is sold in one frame (consumer / B2B / enterprise / dev tool / indie) but addressed to a customer who lives in a different frame.

**Diagnostic question:** Imagine the named customer reading the offer cold. Does the trust bar, the language, and the price feel native to their buying context?

**Bad version:**

> *Customer:* CFOs at mid-market companies evaluating financial software.
> *Proof:* "Featured on Product Hunt and trending on Hacker News."
> *Guarantee:* "Try it free for 14 days, cancel anytime."

**Why it breaks:** A CFO's buying context is procurement, security review, SOC2, vendor references, and an annual contract. Product Hunt rankings and a 14-day SaaS trial are signals from a different planet — they actively *erode* trust by making the offer feel unserious for the buyer's context. Frame mismatch is fatal because it's almost invisible to founders embedded in their own product.

**Good version (Linear's frame discipline)**:Linear's offer is built end-to-end in the frame of a fast-growing software team. Proof points are tools that same customer trusts (Vercel, Ramp, Mercury, Cash App); review evidence comes from G2 (where software teams shop), not the Mac App Store; the guarantee is built around team-level free seats (not a personal trial). Every line stays in the same buying context the customer already lives in.

## How to use this document

Run this checklist after a first draft of your Product Offer. If any of the eight patterns are present, treat that section as unfinished — every downstream artifact (Customer Persona, Business Strategy, Market Validation, growth plan, ad copy) inherits the weakness.

Name the pattern when you spot it. Naming is half the cure: "this is a Frame Mismatch" gives you and your team a shared vocabulary for the fix, instead of the vaguer "this just feels off."

The patterns compound. A Wide Wedge usually causes a Vague Pain (because no specific person has a specific pain), which causes an Unmeasurable Outcome, which causes a Toothless Guarantee. Fixing the wedge often clears two or three patterns downstream — so when more than one pattern shows up, start at the top of the offer and work down.

Re-check after major strategic changes. Personas decay; offers decay faster. Every new wedge, price change, or competitor move should trigger a fresh pass through these eight patterns.
