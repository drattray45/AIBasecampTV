# Customer Persona Framework

> **Drafted:** June 2026 — AIBasecamp.TV (pre-launch). **Persona:** Diane — the Lapped Veteran. Built from the locked Product Offer + reasoned priors; web search was unavailable this session, so named tools/prices/communities are tagged `[confirm live]` and all `[validate]` sections are hypotheses to confirm in the first 90 days of signups + 3–5 customer interviews. This is the **primary** persona; two secondaries (the near-retirement "content" persona and the freelancer/owner "high-intent" persona) are noted at the foot.

**Persona:** Diane — the Lapped Veteran
**Tagline:** A 49-year-old marketing/operations manager with 15 years of earned competence, quietly terrified the 29-year-olds are speaking a language she doesn't — with the income and the motive to fix it, and too proud to admit the gap at work.

> **Governing rule (carry into all copy): fire the fear in public, resolve it in private.** The dread fires at work (incognito tab between meetings / LinkedIn, where status anxiety is socially sayable) — that's where hooks and Shorts live. The purchase happens at home (9pm kitchen / Facebook, where shame drops from acute to manageable and Scout shows up as the safe guide). Sell at the work moment and you lose her to panic; only ever show the kitchen moment and you never light the fear that drives her there.

## Header

*Who is this persona, in one 2 sentences?*

> Good: role-based name + tagline that names the job and the obstacle, dated and sourced
> Bad: demographic archetype ("millennial mom"), undated, no evidence base

**Persona:** Diane — the Lapped Veteran
**Tagline:** A tenured, respected mid-career manager watching colleagues half her age casually "have AI do it," who has both the money and the motive to fix it but is too proud to admit at work that she doesn't know where to start.

## 1. Identity

*What role are they when the problem shows up?*

> Good: a job role or life context (e.g. "solo bookkeeper for trades businesses")
> Bad: a job title with no context, or a personality trait

**Role:** Marketing or operations manager at a 60–400-person company, 15+ years' tenure, manages 2–6 people.

**One-line description:** Respected, competent, *not* clerical — costed up from "admin" on purpose. She has runway (years of career to protect), agency (re-skilling actually changes her trajectory), and disposable income at the career stage most willing to spend on staying relevant. Sole decider on her own learning; pays personally, never expenses it.

---

## 2. Problem Context

*When and where does the problem show up?*

> Good: a specific moment, place, device, and mood
> Bad: "all the time", "at work", "on their phone"

**When does the problem come up?** Tuesday ~2pm, just out of a team meeting where a 29-year-old associate said "oh, I just had ChatGPT draft all the variants" and everyone nodded — so she nodded too.

**Where (physical setting, device, time of day)?** Two rooms, one fear. *Public form:* back at her desk, an **incognito tab** so no one sees her search history, typing a vague question, getting a bland answer, closing it. *Private form (the buying moment):* **9pm at the kitchen island**, laptop, kids settled, googling "how to actually use ChatGPT for marketing" where it's safe to not-know.

**What's the emotional state?** At work: fight-or-flight, exposed, hiding. At home: the shame has dropped from acute to manageable — quiet enough to act on. (You do not get a purchase out of someone in fight-or-flight.)

**Who else is around?** At work, the younger colleagues she's afraid of. At home, no one watching — which is exactly why she can finally admit she needs to deal with it.

---

## 3. Behaviors & Habits

*What do they actually do, not what they say they'd do?*

> Good: observed, repeatable, includes the workaround they're embarrassed about
> Bad: aspirational, "they should…", best-practice descriptions

**What do they do repeatedly to solve the problem?** Googles the same question repeatedly, opens a YouTube tutorial that loses her in 90 seconds, started a LinkedIn Learning course and didn't finish, and mostly hopes it blows over.

**What workarounds have they built?** Uses **incognito mode** so her search history doesn't betray the ops manager who doesn't know what a prompt is. Has *once* quietly asked the one younger colleague she trusts — and felt the sting of having to. `[validate]`

---

## 4. Tools & Tech Fluency

*What's already in their stack, and how do they treat new tools?*

> Good: named apps, last adoption with stick/drop outcome, honest AI trust level
> Bad: "tech savvy", "uses AI", generic comfort labels

**Apps and tools they currently use:** `[confirm live]` Outlook, Excel, Teams/Slack, Canva, LinkedIn, Google Workspace, iPhone; Asana or Monday for projects; Mailchimp/HubSpot if marketing. She is fluent in all of it — the issue is not general tech ability.

**Last new tool adopted (and did it stick?):** Canva stuck (accessible, non-threatening). A LinkedIn Learning course on AI did *not* stick — abandoned within two sessions because it assumed a starting point she didn't have.

**Comfort level with AI tools:** Low. Opened ChatGPT free once or twice, underwhelmed.

**Trust level for AI output:** Untested and anxious — **she can't yet tell a good answer from a confident-wrong one**, which is exactly why she won't risk it on anything that matters at work.

---

## 5. Job-to-be-Done

*What are they hiring a solution to do for them?*

> Good: situation, motivation, and outcome that a stranger could re-recognize
> Bad: feature wishlist, demographic statement

**When** my younger colleagues casually use AI in meetings and I'm nodding along,
**I want to** quietly learn to use these tools for my real work,
**So I can** stop feeling exposed and protect a career I'm not ready to leave.

---

## 6. Triggers

*What specific event sends them looking for a fix?*

> Good: a nameable event with a frequency and a measurable cost
> Bad: "they get frustrated sometimes"

**What specific event causes them to search for a solution?** A meeting where a younger colleague demos AI; a job posting in her field now listing "AI tools" as a requirement; a company "AI initiative" announcement; a performance review that mentions "AI fluency."

**How often does this happen?** Weekly and rising. `[validate]`

**What's the cost (time, money, emotional)?** Low cash, high cortisol — wasted evenings and a creeping, compounding sense of obsolescence she can't say out loud.

---

## 7. Current Alternatives

*What are they doing today instead of using you?*

> Good: a specific tool, spreadsheet, person, or ritual + a satisfaction score
> Bad: "nothing", "they just deal with it"

**What are they doing today to handle the job?** Google + abandoned YouTube tutorials + one started-not-finished LinkedIn Learning course + hoping it blows over.

**How long have they been doing it that way?** ~6–12 months of low-grade avoidance. `[validate]`

**How satisfied are they (1-10) with the current approach?** **2/10.**

---

## 8. Pains & Frustrations

*What hurts enough to act on?*

> Good: each pain is specific enough that one design decision could fix it
> Bad: "it's clunky", "it's slow", "the UX is bad"

**Pain 1:** Every "just use AI" resource assumes a starting point she doesn't have — they begin at step 3. (Design fix: the Basecamp Path's frictionless home-turf task 1.)

**Pain 2:** She can't judge whether an AI answer is *right*, so she can't trust it for anything that matters — the trust gap, not a skill gap.

**Pain 3:** Asking for help means admitting the gap to people whose respect is her professional capital, so she stays stuck in silence. (Design fix: Scout, a guide who can't look down on her; learning in private.)

---

## 9. Decision Criteria & Objections

*What will they judge a solution on, and what will stop the deal?*

> Good: ranked criteria, the real objection in their words, and concrete proof that beats it
> Bad: "price and quality", "they want it to be good"

**Top 3 things they'll evaluate a solution on:** (1) made for someone like me, not techy; (2) won't make me feel stupid; (3) genuinely useful for my real work, fast.

**The objection that most often kills the deal:** *"Isn't this free on YouTube — and am I just too far behind anyway?"* (Two objections fused: why-pay, and the self-blame verdict.)

**The proof that would overcome that objection:** a peer testimonial from someone her own age and role who felt exactly this and crossed over — worth more than any credential or tech endorsement.

---

## 10. Willingness to Pay

*What is this problem already costing them, and what would they pay to fix it?*

> Good: real numbers, named anchor products, a price ceiling, the budget it comes from
> Bad: "depends", "whatever's fair", "I'd have to ask"

**What they currently spend on the problem (money + time):** ~$0 cash; many unpaid, frustrated evenings.

**Anchor products and their prices:** `[confirmed June 21, 2026]` LinkedIn Learning $39.99/mo (~$240/yr annual); Coursera Plus $59/mo or $399/yr; MasterClass $120/$180/$240/yr; a Udemy course $15–200. (Confirms the $99–199 flagship sits below a year of any subscription anchor — a finishable, owned purchase under her $299 ceiling.)

**Acceptable monthly price:** $19–39/mo, or **$99–199 one-time** for a course.

**Price ceiling (above which they'd say "I'll think about it"):** **~$299.** Above it, a personal-discretionary "yes" turns into a deliberated decision — or a spousal conversation, which reintroduces the very shame she's avoiding.

**Budget bucket (personal / business / discretionary):** **Personal discretionary.** She won't expense it, because expensing it means admitting the gap to her employer.

> **Strategic consequence:** $299 is not a cap on the business — it's the ceiling on what you can charge *Diane* before she stops being Diane. **Flagship lives at $99–$299** sold to her as an individual (payment plan as the pressure-release valve at the top of the range). To sell above $299 you must change *who you're selling to* → the freelancer/owner (deductible business expense, ceiling lifts) or the employer-budget/team path. Don't price the secret personal buyer out of her own brand.

## 11. Watering Holes

*Where do they already gather, and who do they already trust?*

> Good: named subreddits, Discord servers, newsletters, podcasts, individuals
> Bad: "Twitter", "the internet", "industry events"

**Specific communities (name them):** **Facebook- and LinkedIn-native, NOT Reddit/Discord** — Reddit is where the people she's *afraid of* hang out. **Candidate surfaces identified June 21, 2026 (verify composition before trusting):** Facebook tool/identity groups — *5 Minute Bookkeeping with QuickBooks Online*, *QB Power User Community* (closer match than business-building groups, which skew to practice-owners); women's professional networks — *Dreamers & Doers*, *Everywoman* (~30K), *Women in Revenue* (~7K); LinkedIn — *Connect: Professional Women's Network* (~500K). **Channel-mechanics note: LinkedIn removed *followable* hashtags in late 2024** — so LinkedIn = commenting under "AI and jobs/upskilling" posts + posting into the algorithm, not browsing a hashtag feed. **Platform split maps to the two moments: LinkedIn = the work-moment room (fire the fear, fear-led hooks); Facebook = the kitchen-moment room (softer, real emotional language, reassurance-led conversion).** *Caveat: the most-findable groups skew to owners/entrepreneurs, not employed mid-career Dianes — her exact employed-manager channel still needs hunting.*

**Specific podcasts / newsletters / channels (name them):** `[confirm live]` Morning Brew-type business newsletters; mainstream news (NPR/NYT). She is *not* subscribed to AI-creator newsletters.

**People they trust and follow (name them):** `[validate]` **She has no trusted AI guide today** — that vacancy is the brand's entire opening. She trusts mainstream sources, a few LinkedIn voices in her own field, and (reluctantly) her one sharp younger colleague.

## 12. Anti-Persona

*Who looks similar, but is a bad fit you should keep out?*

> Good: a recognizable lookalike, the reason they'd churn, and the gating signal that filters them out
> Bad: "anyone who isn't our customer"

**Who looks similar but is a bad fit?** The eager 28-year-old early-adopter already automating with AI who wants advanced/agent content. (Secondary anti-fit: the "make money with AI" hustle-seeker — wrong intent, wrong brand.)

**Why would those people churn or complain?** They'd find it "too basic / too slow" and leave reviews that scare off the actual buyer — the nervous beginner reads "too basic" as "this is for people ahead of me too."

**How do we keep them from signing up in the first place?** The "for people who feel behind" framing + Scout's deliberate pace self-selects them out before signup; no advanced/automation promises in the copy.

## Secondary personas (named, not built)

- **The near-retirement worker — your *content* persona.** Most emotionally vivid dread → best hooks and Shorts. Watches, relates, shares; buys least (short payback, fatalism, low re-skilling appetite). Carries reach, not revenue.
- **The freelancer/owner — your *high-intent* persona.** Clearest ROI logic, lowest spend friction (deductible business expense), may out-convert everyone once there's data — but it's an outcome/ROI-led sale, not a reassurance-led one, so it doesn't sit at the center of a "you're not behind" brand. Carries the higher-ticket edge.
